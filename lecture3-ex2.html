LECTURE 3 — EX 2: HISTOGRAM CONSTRUCTION

üéØ OBJECTIVE: VISUALIZING RISK STRUCTURE

Our goal is to construct the distribution of Log Returns using the Equal-Width Bins method. Instead of looking at a chaotic time series, we reorganize the data into a frequency distribution. This allows us to visually analyze the true "shape of risk".

üó∫Ô∏è STEP 1: DEFINE BOUNDARIES

Concept: Find the absolute 'Map' of your data. You need to know the worst and best historical days to properly calibrate your chart.

=MIN(return_range)
=MAX(return_range)

Purpose: Defines the full range of observed returns.

üìè STEP 2: CALIBRATE BINS

Concept: We must divide our total return range into equal intervals. We standardly choose n = 30 bins.

Bin Width = (Max - Min) / n
=(max - min) / 30

Logic: Ensures every bar in your histogram has exactly the same width.

ü™ú STEP 3: CONSTRUCT BIN BOUNDARIES (THE LADDER)

Create equally spaced intervals from the minimum to the maximum. These will act as your X-axis categories.

First boundary:
=min
Next boundaries:
=previous_boundary + bin_size

Instruction: Repeat this specific formula down the column until you have n boundaries.

‚öôÔ∏è STEP 4: THE FREQUENCY ENGINE

This function counts how many daily return observations fall into each specific "bucket".

=FREQUENCY(return_range ; bin_boundaries_range)

Optional percentages = use =frequency / total_number_of_observations.

üìä STEP 5: VISUALIZATION

  1. Select your calculated Bins and Frequencies.
  2. Go to Insert → Column Chart.
  3. The Secret Trick: Right-click any bar on the chart, go to Format Data Series.
  4. Set Gap Width = 0%.
Note: A true histogram must have NO space between bars to represent a continuous numerical distribution perfectly.

‚öñÔ∏è ECONOMIC INTERPRETATION

The final histogram acts as a visual X-ray of the asset's behavior over time.

  • Dispersion: How wide is the bell curve? (Measures Volatility).
  • Extreme Values: Are there stray bars far out to the left or right? (Indicates Black Swans / Tail Risk).
  • Shape: Is it perfectly symmetrical like a Normal Distribution, or skewed? (Tells you about market biases).
üìå PRO TIP

We use Log Returns because they are additive, making the histogram mathematically consistent and robust when we analyze different time horizons (like scaling daily risk to monthly risk)!
