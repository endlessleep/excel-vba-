EX 5: PORTFOLIO CONSTRUCTION & ANALYSIS

‚öñÔ∏è 1. BUILDING AN EQUALLY WEIGHTED PORTFOLIO

Objective: Construct a portfolio where every single stock has the exact same importance.

  1. Step 1: Count the Stocks (N)
    =COUNT(range_of_tickers)

    Logic: COUNT ignores text (names) and only counts cells with numbers (like returns or prices) to give you the total number of assets, N.

  2. Step 2: Calculate Individual Weight (wi)
    wi = 1 / N
    =1 / COUNT(range)

    Logic: If you have 10 stocks, each receives exactly 10% of your total capital (1/10). Simply copy this value across all stock columns!

  3. Step 3: The Sum Constraint
    Σ wi = 1
    Excel Tip to avoid rounding errors!
    Instead of calculating the final stock's weight using 1/N, force it to close perfectly at 100%:
    =1 - SUM(all_other_weights)

üìà 2. SLOPE & RSQ (THE BETA ENGINE)

Objective: Measure the precise mathematical relationship between a single stock and the broader market (usually S&P 500) using monthly returns.

MODULE A: THE BETA (β)

Definition: Intensity. Measures the sensitivity of a stock to market variations.

β = Cov(Ri , Rm) / Var(Rm)
// SLOPE is mathematically identical to Cov/Var! =SLOPE(stock_returns ; market_returns)
Interpretation:
  • β > 1: Aggressive (More volatile than market)
  • β = 1: Matches the market precisely
  • β < 1: Defensive (Less volatile than market)
  • β < 0: Moves opposite to the market

MODULE B: THE R-SQUARED (R²)

Definition: Quality. Measures the proportion of the stock's variations that are directly explained by the market.

R² = Corr(Ri , Rm)²
=RSQ(stock_returns ; market_returns)
Interpretation Example:
If R² = 0.60, it means:
→ 60% of variations are explained by the market (Systematic Risk).
→ 40% come from the company's own news/events (Specific/Idiosyncratic Risk).

üîó 3. PORTFOLIO RETURN (SUMPRODUCT)

Objective: Calculate the return of the entire portfolio by merging the returns of the individual stocks with their respective weights. Wait... SUMPRODUCT does not take a simple average! It takes a Weighted Average.

Financial Formula:
Rp = Σ (wi × Ri)
The Excel Power Tool:
// Multiplies each return by its weight and sums all the products: =SUMPRODUCT(range_returns ; range_weights)
Critical Technical Rules
  • 1. Alignment: Both ranges must strictly have the exact same size and align column by column (match stock to stock).
  • 2. Master Locking ($): You MUST lock the weights range (e.g., $B$2:$K$2).
    Why? Because your target weights stay constant every period, while the observed returns change every month as you drag the formula down!

EXTENSION: PORTFOLIO PROPERTIES

üîπ 1) Weighted Average Beta

üìå Financial Formula
βportfolio = Σ (wi × βi)
  • wi: Weight of asset i in the portfolio
  • βi: Beta of asset i
  • The portfolio beta is the exact weighted average of individual betas.
üìå Excel Structure
=SUMPRODUCT(range_betas ; range_weights)
  • range_betas: Row containing individual stock betas
  • range_weights: Row containing portfolio weights
Important: The weights range MUST be locked with absolute references ($) if the formula is copied.
üìå Economic Interpretation

Portfolio beta measures the portfolio‚Äôs sensitivity to market movements.

  • βportfolio > 1: Portfolio is more volatile than the market
  • βportfolio = 1: Same risk as the market
  • βportfolio < 1: Less volatile than the market
Core CAPM Concept:
E(Rp) = rf + βp × (E(Rm) - rf)
Beta determines the expected return required by investors. This measure is theoretically exact.

üîπ 2) Weighted Average R-Squared

üìå Financial Formula
R²portfolio ≈ Σ (wi × R²i)
  • wi: Weight of asset i
  • R²i: R-squared of asset i from regression on the market
üìå Excel Structure
=SUMPRODUCT(range_r_squared ; range_weights)
  • range_r_squared: Row containing individual R² values
  • range_weights: Row containing portfolio weights (fixed with $)
üìå Economic Interpretation

R-squared measures the proportion of a stock‚Äôs return variance explained by market movements.
For the portfolio:

  • A higher weighted R² suggests the portfolio behaves more like the market.
  • A lower R² suggests more idiosyncratic (firm-specific) risk.
‚ö†Ô∏è Important Conceptual Note

Unlike beta, the weighted average R² is descriptive. It is not a fundamental CAPM parameter.
The true portfolio R² would require computing:

R² = 1 - (Variancespecific / Variancetotal)

So the weighted average is a simplified indicator, not a theoretical identity.

PRO TIP

A stock can have a high Beta but a low R-squared. This means it reacts strongly to the market, but most of its movements are still caused by its own internal news!
